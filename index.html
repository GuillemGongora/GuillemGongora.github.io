About

Guillem Góngora Moral (1994) is a composer and musician based in Rotterdam. His artistic work ranges from pieces for instruments with and without electronics, sound installations and music for the moving body.

He studied composition at ESMUC (Barcelona) with Bernat Vivancos and Mauricio Sotelo, and piano with Xavier Pardo and Xavier Barbeta. After obtaining his bachelor’s degree, he moved to the Netherlands to study his master’s at HKU (Utrecht) incorporating the use of electronics in his work. In 2019, while in his master’s, he was awarded with the 17th International Prize Joan Guinjoan from Barcelona with his piece Processus. Shortly after graduating, he started collaborating with different dancers creating the music for several dance performances. He created the music for the performance Out of the blue by Strange Strangers which toured around the Netherlands, Belgium, Spain, Poland and Italy. Out of the blue won the award for best choreography in 25Masdanza (Gran Canaria), was semifinalist at Rotterdam International Duet Choreography Competition, and finalist at Festival 10 Sentidos (Valencia) and Copenhagen International Choreography Competition.

He has received masterclasses from Stefano Gervasoni, Sofia Gubaidulina, Mauro Lanza, Mauricio Rodríguez, Raphael Cëndo, Clara Maïda, Bryn Harrison, Kaija Saariaho and Ondrej Adámek. Also, his music has been played by ensemble BCN216 (Barcelona), Curious Chamber Players (Stockholm) and Insomnio Ensemble (Utrecht) amongst others.

Besides his artistic work, Guillem has given masterclasses and workshops in Barcelona, Tenerife and Rotterdam, about composition and music for dance.

Double-edged circle

Double-edged circle

For choreography
2020

Double-edged circle is based on the transformation idea about the fluidity and volatility of ourselves and the capacity to recognize the evil of our nature: the Jungian idea of accepting your own shadow, the unconscious aspect of the personality, which sometimes can be dark and even frightening. Repetitiveness that is slowly developing, constantly exchanging between the different disciplines, and influencing both ways represents the interconnectedness between positive and negative, the need to be both in order to reach a balance. Double-edged circle is a contradiction about needing the two opposites in order to complete the circle.

Idea and choreography by Gabrielle Emily Aidulis.

Out of the blue

Out of the blue

For choreography
2019

Out of the blue revolves around the reality of dreams. Danielle and Alexandra explore their reality by using the anxiety one can feel within a dream as an entry point for their creation. During the piece, they transmit the uncertainty and difficulty they have in facing the unknown. This is achieved by researching the REM sleep (Rapid Eye Movement) and how this stage affects their experience. By doing so, the audience joins them in their journey of intensity and rest, letting everyone be taken by their unconsciousness. Through conveying this experience, Danielle and Alexandra bring awareness on how we deal with confronting reality.
Out of the blue received the jury prize for best choreography in 25Masdanza (Gran Canaria), was semifinalist at Rotterdam International Duet Choreography Competition, and finalist at Festival 10 Sentidos (Valencia) and Copenhagen International Choreography Competition.

二首中國詩 [Two Chinese poems]

For double bass, percussion and live electronics
2018

二首中国诗 [Two Chinese Poems] is a two movement piece, 花非花 [The bloom is not a bloom] and 櫻花落盡階前月 [Beneath the moon, before the steps, all cherry blossom has fallen], inspired by the two homonymous poems.
The poems are taken as an inspiration for the music, grasping the movement, tempo, images of the words and converting them into sound.

... will always get through

For viola and soundscape
2017

... will always get through was inspired by a black chapter in the history of the Netherlands, the Nazi bombing of Rotterdam. Thus, the electronics depict the procedure of an air raid: sirens to announce the imminent threat, fighters and bombers coming from northeast and south (like in the original bombing) and dropping tons of bombs behind them, and an “all clear” signal at the end. The viola, as an individual, frames such terrible scene and interacts with that soundscape in a manner that comprises quiet, trembling sounds and loud, strident ones.
The title of this piece refers to a phrase used by Stanley Baldwin in 1932 in the speech “A Fear for the Future” to the British Parliament. He and others believed that, regardless of air defenses, sufficient bomber aircraft would survive to destroy cities. And, unfortunately, he was right.

Processus

Processus

For ensemble (2 flutes, 2 Bb clarinets, bass clarinet, 2 horns, piano, 2 violins, 2 cellos and double bass)
2016
Winner of the XVII Joan Guinjoan international award for young composers

With Processus, a piece of musical exploration composed in 2016, Guillem Góngora sought to reflect his need for musical exploration, which led him to experiment with computer-generated material. The piece is structured with five different textures which, in the words of the composer, “are affected by transformational processes which jumble them together like a complicated puzzle”.
During the composition process, Góngora explains that he went through a period where he lacked ideas and felt alienated, probably because he was working with material and tools that had never been used before, as well as other non-music related reasons. That is why the title, Processus, also expresses “the path that finally led to the piece in question”.

Guillem Gongora · Processus

12 Preludis

12 Preludis

For 2 pianos tuned a quarter tone apart
2014

This work responds to a need of learning and distancing from a conservatoire-like, classical music aesthetic and writing. Thus, these twelve preludes are an approximation and experimentation on a 24-equal-tempered tuning system. However, every one of them represents a particularity or idea linked to that musical system related to psychoacoustics and nature of sound. Composers like Ives, Hába or Wyschnegradsky already went deep into this territory and reached similar conclusions on the behavior of sound and listener perception. These preludes put into practice these and other discoveries.
Finally, thanks to my composition teacher, Bernat Vivancos, for the support and guiding.
